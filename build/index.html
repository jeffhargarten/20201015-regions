ELECTION

Once regional, early voting has swept across Minnesota

By Jeff Hargarten
Star Tribune October 16, 2020

As Minnesotans request a record 2 million absentee ballots before Election Day on Nov. 3, it’s clear early voting has transformed from being more common in the state’s sparsely-populated regions to a staple of political engagement during a time of global pandemic.

Early voting has expanded wildly compared to the last two statewide elections, in urban, suburban and rural counties alike, transcending typical cultural distinctions between residents of small towns and big cities.

Despite partisan disagreements over the safety and validity of mail-voting, both Republican and Democratic areas of the state have seen unprecedented growth in advanced voting, narrowing the window for the trajectories of political races to change as October wanes.

Here are some key ways use of no-excuses absentee voting has evolved in Minnesota and what implications it may have for voter enthusiasm and overall turnout during a high-stakes election:

2014 general election

Minnesota has used mail-in voting since before World War II. But 2014's midterms and gubernatorial election provided the state's first foray into its newly-approved absentee voting law, which lifted the requirement for residents to provide reasons they needed to vote early.

But public awareness had yet to fully catch on, so the highest rates of adoption remained confined to sparsely-populated regions in northern and western Minnesota where many mail-only precincts resided.

2016 general election

Use of early voting in Minnesota jumped considerably during the 2016 general election, the first presidential year where no-excuses absentee balloting was available, and nearly a quarter of total voters took advantage.

Early balloting as a percentage of overall voting increased across the state, with more than a half million mail-in and in-person advanced votes being cast – though rural counties maintained the highest rates.

2018 general election

While 2018's gubernatorial race drew a similar percentage of absentee ballots as 2016, it played a significant role in boosting Minnesota’s midterm voter turnout, which had been sliding since the turn of the century.

Higher rates of absentee voting continued to increase in northern Minnesota counties and those along its western edge. For instance, among the dozen counties comprising northwestern Minnesota, about 40 percent of voters cast absentee ballots.

Meanwhile, adoption in places like Hennepin and Ramsey was stagnant, floating below 30 percent of their combined ballots.

2020 state primaries

So far this year, the COVID-19 pandemic has caused a dramatic shift in how Minnesotans vote, with 6-in-10 of the state’s primary vote arriving absentee, all but five percent via mail.

The sudden exponential expansion of early voting also accompanied a huge increase in turnout, as more Minnesotans cast absentee ballots than the entirety of 2016’s state primary vote. This led to higher early voting rates spreading across the state, including in the Twin Cities metro area where adoption had been relatively slower.

And even amid concerns of the system being strained under the influx of mail-in ballots, relatively few were rejected or arrived late.

2020 general election

With less than three weeks remaining before this year’s general election, nearly a million absentee ballots have been sent back by voters and accepted – and they’re coming from nearly everywhere across Minnesota.

Some of the largest rate increases are still in rural counties, though notably less so in many of those northwestern counties where adoption was already very high.

At the current pace, absentee balloting alone is set to shatter turnout records in Minnesota and could alone seal the electoral outcomes of many races up and down the ballot.

Partisan implications

The massive spike in absentee balloting could also indicate significant voter enthusiasm this year, particularly among those who usually vote for Democrats.

In Minnesota’s state primary this year, the half-million absentee ballots cast favored a big increase in Democratic turnout. For instance, in Minnesota’s U.S. Senate primary, the number of Democratic votes more than doubled Republican totals.

Additionally, about 40 percent of the entire primary vote was comprised of just absentee ballots from the nine counties Hillary Clinton carried to narrowly win Minnesota in 2016. This year, those same counties represent almost two-thirds of accepted absentee ballots ahead of Nov. 3.

Though Democrats are also more likely to vote early. A recent Minnesota Poll found 61 percent of those surveyed intend to vote on Election Day, including just 46 percent of Democrats, while 78 percent of Republicans would wait to vote in person.
